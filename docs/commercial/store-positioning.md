# Store Positioning — Working Copy

## One-sentence pitch

**[Primary title]: Thestra no Jijou** is a compact, replayable survival dungeon
RPG where you raise named summons, survive unstable expeditions, and decide
what you are willing to sacrifice to descend deeper.

## Short description

Descend beneath Thestra in a dark turn-based dungeon RPG. Summon strange
creatures, raise them through dangerous expeditions, and know when to turn
back—because death is permanent, escape is limited, and the creatures you
sacrifice may be the only way forward.

## Long-description structure

1. **Descent:** Procedural layouts make each expedition unstable, but the
   dungeon's key mysteries are deliberately authored.
2. **Pacts:** Build a roster of individually named summons with different
   combat roles, promotion paths, item-creation strengths, and costs.
3. **Survival:** Return before resources, distance, and traps leave no way out.
4. **Sacrifice:** Trade a creature for conditional materials and returned EXP;
   choose carefully, because combat death is permanent too.
5. **Thestra:** Return to a town of other summoners and complicated people while
   a deeper story gradually emerges.

## Tag direction

Primary: Dungeon Crawler; Turn-Based Combat; Dark Fantasy; RPG; Procedural
Generation; Creature Collector; Perma Death; Atmospheric; Singleplayer.

Secondary, only if truthfully supported by the final build: Traditional
Roguelike; Story Rich; Horror; Pixel Graphics; Difficult; JRPG; Party-Based RPG;
Multiple Endings.

Do not use **Roguelite** as a leading tag or phrase while the game has no
game-over meta progression.

## Trailer beats

Target: 45–60 seconds. Gameplay should begin immediately.

1. Town and title: “Beneath Thestra, every pact has a cost.”
2. Fast creature introductions: art, name, role, and a strong combat moment.
3. Resource pressure: deeper path, dwindling return option, trap or sealed room.
4. Ritual: summon, promotion, or sacrifice UI shown clearly enough to read.
5. Loss: a named creature falls or a sacrifice confirmation appears.
6. Mystery: a townsperson/rival line and an image of the antagonist's presence.
7. End card: title, “Coming Soon,” and wishlist call to action.

## Capsule direction

The capsule must communicate a dark creature-summoning dungeon RPG at thumbnail
size. Include:

- one legible Summoner silhouette or focal human figure;
- one strange, memorable summon;
- descent, ritual, or dungeon-depth imagery;
- high-value contrast rather than a busy ensemble;
- the title rendered clearly enough for a small Steam capsule.

Avoid relying only on an invented-word title, a generic dark castle, or a large
cast of tiny sprites.

## Content pillars

### 1. Mechanical grief

- “I could save my strongest summon—or use it to unlock the next one.”
- “This creature is weak. I raised it anyway.”
- “I had enough to go deeper, but not enough to return.”
- “A dungeon RPG teaches you when to leave.”

### 2. Imagined lost-game artifacts

- Manual fragments and creature notes.
- Fake 1990s magazine copy or screenshots.
- A playthrough/blog fragment describing a rare situation.
- Brief, intentional faux-localization instruction screens.

### 3. Human circumstances

- Town characters with sharp, specific dialogue.
- Other Summoners' contrasting ethics and methods.
- Glimpses of the rival's changing story.

Mechanical grief sells the game. The other pillars create identity and return
attention. Never let an artifact post obscure the actual game footage and
wishlist path.
