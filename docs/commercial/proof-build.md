# Proof Build Brief

## Question

Can a new player understand the survival loop, become attached to a generated
creature, and leave wanting to return to both the town and the dungeon?

## Non-negotiable player arc

1. Arrive in the town and meet one memorable towns-person.
2. Receive or summon a first creature from a small, legible choice.
3. Enter an unstable dungeon layout with a visible route back.
4. Learn that resources and distance make continuing dangerous.
5. Make a meaningful choice: retreat, risk a deeper event, or use a return
   resource.
6. Experience one creature identity beat: skill use, promotion preview,
   composition reaction, or item-creation value.
7. Encounter a loss/sacrifice decision that is understandable and reversible
   only through future play, not an immediate undo.
8. Return to town for a changed conversation and an unanswered mystery beat.

## Scope

### Town

- One dense hub screen or area.
- Three interactive townspeople: relationship anchor, practical service, and
  rival or rival lead.
- One optional relationship interaction not framed as a quest.
- One explicit return/rest function.

### Dungeon

- One procedural tileset plus authored or semi-fixed event rooms.
- One trap/blocked-return situation.
- One visible but risky deeper reward.
- One recruitment or creature-related event.
- One short combat encounter and one pressure encounter.

### Creature set

- 8–12 usable creatures.
- At least one cheap starter, one immediately strong option, one weak but
  appealing growth path, one item-creation specialist, and one risky utility
  option.
- Distinct art, flavor, and skill-role messaging for every creature in the set.
- History fields stored even if only a small subset is visible: generated name,
  species, level, promotion, expedition count, battle losses, cause of death,
  and sacrifice status.

### Required systems

- Summoning with clear cost.
- Party/reserve management.
- Limited return method and a legible path back.
- Combat death.
- Sacrifice that cannot empty the party.
- Game-over state with a clear, respectful explanation.
- Save/load safety sufficient for external playtests.

## Test questions

Ask after play, without leading the answer:

1. What were you trying to do when you entered the dungeon?
2. When did you decide it was time to leave, if you did?
3. Which creature do you remember, and why?
4. What did you think sacrifice was for?
5. Why did your expedition end?
6. What would you do differently next time?
7. What do you think is wrong with Thestra?

## Failure diagnoses

| Observation | Likely problem | First response |
|---|---|---|
| Players never retreat voluntarily | Retreat is under-rewarded or danger is unreadable | Improve return cues and escalating pressure |
| Players sacrifice without hesitation | Creature identity or sacrifice value is too abstract | Improve history, art, growth anticipation, and cost framing |
| Players refuse to sacrifice ever | Sacrifice feels like a trap | Give it a visible, desirable, but non-mandatory outcome |
| Players die without understanding why | Information/UI problem, not productive difficulty | Improve forecast, logs, warnings, and map feedback |
| Players remember no towns-person | The hub lacks a sharp authored scene | Add a specific relationship or conflict beat |
| Players care only about combat efficiency | Narrative and creature texture are not surfacing during play | Add flavor, reactions, and return-state variation |
